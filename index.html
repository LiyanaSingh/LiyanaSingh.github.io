About Me

My engineering identity is rooted in applying technical knowledge with the intention to improve lives. When I applied to university, I was driven by the idea of helping people and knew that my strengths in maths and physics could be put to good use in engineering. I've always been drawn to mechatronics and biomedical engineering, where innovation meets care using technology to solve health-related challenges.

This identity has been shaped by both academic and practical experiences. My current research investigating lifting techniques using trajectory optimisation has deepened my interest in biomechanics and how engineering can enhance human performance. I also completed vacation work at Impulse Biomedical, where I worked alongside a team developing medical devices aimed at increasing accessibility in under-resourced communities. This experience made the social impact of engineering feel tangible and strengthened my commitment to biomedical innovation.

I increasingly see myself not just as a student of engineering, but as someone who thinks like an engineer: analytical, creative, and driven by purpose. More than anything, I see engineering as a tool for improving quality of life. I feel most like an engineer when I'm solving meaningful problems and contributing to something bigger than myself.

Projects

MicroMouse

A micro-mouse is a wheeled robot designed to navigate through a maze using sensors and control algorithms. The goal is for the robot to autonomously find the most efficient route to the center of a maze. In this project, the maze was constructed physically using wooden walls and black tape marking the floor, and the robot used infrared sensors to detect the walls and floor lines. It has an STM32 microcontroller onboard that it uses to make decisions based on what it senses, and to map out the maze in real time to solve it. I worked on both the hardware (sensors and motors etc) and the software (algorithm).

Penguin Remote Pulse Oximeter

This project was undertaken as part of the EEE4113F Design Class of 2025. African Penguins are critically endangered and are projected to become extinct by 2035 if effective conservation measures are not urgently implemented. Our group worked with BirdLife South Africa to develop a system capable of monitoring the body condition and molt stage of African Penguins.

The solution was a lightweight remote pulse oximeter that monitors the pulse and blood oxygen saturation of African Penguins to understand how their condition varies throughout the molt to inform ecological assessments, benefiting researchers, wildlife managers, and conservationists while ensuring that the birds are not disturbed during the molt.
